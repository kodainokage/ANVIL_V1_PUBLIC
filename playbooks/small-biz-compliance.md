## ANVIL Workforce Services — Practitioner Guide v1.0

**Purpose:** This playbook enables a trained compliance specialist to audit a small business for every required permit, license, registration, and tax obligation at the federal, state, county, and city level — then deliver a clear action plan the client can follow.

**Time to first client:** 3-5 days of study, then same-week delivery. Read the intake script, run the AI prompts against the client's specifics, verify against official sources, and deliver the compliance package.

**Average client value:** $150-400 per engagement. Repeat business from annual renewals, new locations, and referrals.

## 1. Intake Script

**Setting:** Phone, video call, or in-person. Allow 20-30 minutes. Have the client bring any existing business documents, lease agreements, or prior correspondence with government agencies.

**Opening:**

> "Thanks for reaching out. I help small business owners figure out exactly what permits, licenses, and registrations they need to operate legally — and I map out the steps so nothing gets missed. I'm going to ask you a series of questions so I can build your custom compliance checklist. There are no dumb questions on your end either — if something doesn't make sense, stop me."

### Section A: Business Identity

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| A1 | What is the legal name of your business? | Needed for every registration lookup |
| A2 | Do you have a DBA (doing-business-as) or trade name? | Some jurisdictions require separate DBA registration |
| A3 | What is your business structure? (Sole proprietorship, LLC, S-corp, C-corp, partnership, nonprofit) | Structure determines tax obligations, filing requirements, and liability exposure |
| A4 | When did you form the entity, or are you still planning to? | If not yet formed, formation becomes step one |
| A5 | Do you already have an EIN (Employer Identification Number)? | If yes, confirm it. If no, this is an early task. Sole props without employees can use SSN but EIN is better practice. |
| A6 | Is this a new business, an existing business you're buying, or a conversion from a side hustle? | Acquisitions inherit compliance obligations. Conversions may have back-compliance issues. |

### Section B: Location & Geography

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| B1 | What state do you operate in? | State determines business registration, tax ID, and professional license requirements |
| B2 | What city and county? | City and county each have their own business license, zoning, and permit requirements |
| B3 | Are you home-based or in a commercial space? | Home-based businesses face zoning restrictions, HOA rules, and sometimes need a home occupation permit |
| B4 | If commercial: do you have a signed lease? What is the zoning classification of the property? | Wrong zoning = shut down. The lease should specify permitted use. |
| B5 | Do you operate from multiple locations or serve clients at their location? | Multiple locations = multiple city/county licenses in many jurisdictions |
| B6 | Do you sell to customers in other states? (online, wholesale, shipped products) | Triggers nexus analysis for sales tax in other states |
| B7 | Do you have or plan to have a physical sign? | Many cities require a sign permit. Size, illumination, and placement are regulated. |

### Section C: Industry & Operations

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| C1 | What does your business do? Describe it like you're explaining to a neighbor. | Plain language description reveals the regulatory category |
| C2 | What industry or sector? (food, construction, healthcare, professional services, retail, tech, personal services, manufacturing) | Each industry has specific federal and state regulators |
| C3 | Do you handle, prepare, or sell food or beverages? | Triggers health department permits, food handler certifications, FDA registration for manufacturers |
| C4 | Do you provide any service that requires a professional license? (cosmetology, plumbing, electrical, accounting, real estate, counseling, massage, etc.) | State professional licensing boards have their own applications, exams, and continuing education |
| C5 | Do you handle hazardous materials, chemicals, or waste? | EPA, state environmental agency, fire marshal all get involved |
| C6 | Do you sell alcohol, tobacco, or firearms? | Federal TTB/ATF + state liquor authority + local permits. Heavy regulation. |
| C7 | Do you collect personal data or health information from customers? | HIPAA, state privacy laws, PCI-DSS for payment card data |

### Section D: Employees & Workforce

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| D1 | How many employees do you have? (W-2 employees, not contractors) | Employee count triggers federal thresholds: 1+ = OSHA, 15+ = EEOC/ADA Title I, 20+ = COBRA, 50+ = FMLA/ACA |
| D2 | Do you use independent contractors? How many? | Misclassification is the #1 audit trigger. Many states (CA, MA, NJ) use strict ABC tests. |
| D3 | What is your total annual payroll? | Determines workers comp premiums and payroll tax obligations |
| D4 | Do any employees handle food, work with children, or provide healthcare? | Background checks, certifications, and specific training requirements apply |
| D5 | Do you plan to hire in the next 12 months? | Upcoming hires change compliance obligations at each threshold |

### Section E: Financial & Tax

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| E1 | What is your annual revenue or projected annual revenue? | Some permits and licenses scale fees by revenue. Some exemptions have revenue thresholds. |
| E2 | Do you collect sales tax? Do you know if your product/service is taxable in your state? | Sales tax nexus and taxability vary by state. SaaS is taxable in some states, exempt in others. |
| E3 | Do you have a business bank account? | Required for LLCs and corps. Recommended for all. Commingling = piercing the corporate veil. |
| E4 | Do you have business insurance? What types? | General liability is nearly universal. Workers comp is mandatory in most states with employees. |
| E5 | Have you ever received a notice, fine, or violation from any government agency? | Outstanding violations must be addressed. May affect new applications. |

### Section F: Known Issues

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| F1 | Is there anything about your business you're worried might not be compliant? | Clients often know something is off. Get it on the table early. |
| F2 | Have you been operating without any licenses or permits? For how long? | Back-compliance is common. Most jurisdictions allow you to come into compliance without penalty if you act proactively. Some don't. |
| F3 | Has a landlord, customer, competitor, or neighbor ever raised a compliance concern? | Complaints trigger inspections. Better to get ahead of it. |

## 2. AI Analysis Prompts

**How to use these prompts:** After completing the intake, plug the client's specific details into these prompt templates and run them through ChatGPT (GPT-4 or later) or Claude. The AI output is your starting draft — you must verify every item against official government websites before including it in the client deliverable.

**Critical rule:** AI-generated compliance information is a research accelerator, not a final answer. Every permit, license, and requirement you include in the client package must link to the official government source where the client (or you) can verify it and apply.

### Prompt 1: Master Compliance Checklist

```
I need a complete compliance checklist for the following small business:

BUSINESS TYPE: [LLC / Sole Proprietorship / S-Corp / C-Corp / Partnership / Nonprofit]
INDUSTRY: [e.g., residential cleaning service]
STATE: [e.g., Texas]
CITY: [e.g., Austin]
COUNTY: [e.g., Travis County]
NUMBER OF EMPLOYEES: [e.g., 3 W-2 employees, 2 contractors]
ANNUAL REVENUE: [e.g., $180,000]
LOCATION TYPE: [home-based / commercial lease / mobile / online-only]
ADDITIONAL DETAILS: [e.g., uses chemical cleaning products, serves residential and commercial clients]

Generate a comprehensive compliance checklist organized by level:
1. FEDERAL — All applicable federal requirements
2. STATE — All [state] requirements for this business type and industry
3. COUNTY — All [county] requirements
4. CITY — All [city] requirements

For each item, include:
- The specific permit, license, or registration name
- The issuing agency and its website
- Estimated cost
- Estimated processing time
- Whether it requires renewal and how often
- Any prerequisites (exam, inspection, background check, etc.)

Flag any item that is time-sensitive or must be obtained before the business can legally operate.
```

### Prompt 2: Permits & Licenses Deep Dive

```
For a [BUSINESS TYPE] operating a [INDUSTRY DESCRIPTION] in [CITY], [STATE]:

List every permit, license, and registration required at the federal, state, county, and city level. Include:

FEDERAL:
- EIN status and requirements
- Any industry-specific federal licenses (FDA, FCC, TTB, ATF, DOT, EPA, OSHA, etc.)
- Federal employer obligations based on [X] employees

STATE ([STATE]):
- Secretary of State registration/filing
- State tax registration (income tax, sales tax, use tax, franchise tax)
- State professional or occupational license (if applicable to [INDUSTRY])
- State employer registrations (unemployment insurance, workers comp, withholding)
- Any state-specific industry permits

COUNTY ([COUNTY]):
- County business license or tax certificate
- County health department permits (if applicable)
- County building/zoning approval

CITY ([CITY]):
- City business license or privilege license
- City zoning compliance or home occupation permit
- Health department permits
- Fire inspection or fire marshal permit
- Signage permit
- Alarm permit (if applicable)
- Any city-specific industry requirements

For each item provide the official application URL or office address, the fee, and the typical turnaround time.
```

### Prompt 3: Tax Registration Requirements

```
I need a complete tax registration analysis for:

BUSINESS STRUCTURE: [e.g., Single-member LLC]
STATE: [e.g., California]
INDUSTRY: [e.g., freelance graphic design]
EMPLOYEES: [e.g., 0 — solo operator]
REVENUE: [e.g., $95,000/year]
SELLS PHYSICAL GOODS: [Yes/No]
SELLS SERVICES: [Yes/No]
SELLS TO OTHER STATES: [Yes/No — if yes, list states]

Determine:
1. Federal tax classification and filing requirements (Schedule C, 1120, 1120-S, 1065, etc.)
2. Estimated quarterly tax payment obligations
3. State income/franchise tax registration and requirements
4. Sales tax: Is the product/service taxable in [STATE]? Sales tax permit required? Rate?
5. If selling to other states: economic nexus thresholds for each state listed (revenue and transaction count thresholds)
6. Payroll tax requirements (if employees): federal withholding, FICA, FUTA, state withholding, state unemployment (SUTA), local payroll taxes
7. Any industry-specific tax obligations (excise tax, use tax, gross receipts tax)
8. Important tax deadlines and filing dates for this business type

Provide the registration portal URL for each tax type.
```

### Prompt 4: Insurance Requirements Analysis

```
Analyze the insurance requirements for:

BUSINESS TYPE: [e.g., LLC]
INDUSTRY: [e.g., personal training / fitness coaching]
STATE: [e.g., Florida]
EMPLOYEES: [e.g., 2]
LOCATION: [e.g., rented studio space]
ANNUAL REVENUE: [e.g., $120,000]
CLIENT INTERACTION: [e.g., in-person physical training with clients]

Determine:
1. Is general liability insurance legally required? If not, is it practically required? (landlords, clients, contracts)
2. Is workers compensation insurance required in [STATE] for [X] employees? Any exemptions?
3. Is professional liability / errors & omissions insurance required or recommended?
4. Are there industry-specific insurance requirements? (e.g., malpractice for healthcare, pollution liability for contractors, product liability for manufacturers)
5. Is commercial auto insurance needed?
6. Is cyber liability insurance needed? (if handling customer data)
7. What does the lease require? (most commercial leases require GL with the landlord as additional insured)
8. Typical coverage amounts and estimated annual premiums for each type
9. Is a surety bond required for this business type in [STATE]?

For each insurance type, explain what it covers and provide estimated annual premium ranges.
```

### Prompt 5: Zoning Compliance Check

```
Check zoning compliance for:

BUSINESS TYPE: [e.g., home-based bakery / cottage food operation]
ADDRESS TYPE: [residential / commercial / mixed-use]
CITY: [e.g., Portland]
STATE: [e.g., Oregon]
ZONING DISTRICT: [e.g., R-5 residential, if known — if unknown, say "unknown"]
ACTIVITIES: [e.g., baking goods at home, selling at farmers markets and online, occasional customer pickup at home, no signage]

Determine:
1. Is this business activity permitted in this zoning district?
2. Is a home occupation permit required? What are the conditions? (no employees, no signage, no customer traffic, etc.)
3. Are there parking requirements?
4. Are there restrictions on hours of operation?
5. Are there restrictions on deliveries or commercial vehicles?
6. Is a conditional use permit or variance needed?
7. What is the application process and cost for any required zoning permits?
8. Are there any state-level preemptions that override local zoning? (e.g., cottage food laws in many states)
9. What are the consequences of operating without proper zoning approval?

Provide the city planning/zoning department contact info and relevant municipal code sections.
```

## 3. Compliance Requirements by Business Type

### 3A: Food Service / Restaurant

| Category | Typical Requirements |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Federal** | EIN, FDA food facility registration (if manufacturing/processing), OSHA compliance |
| **State** | Business registration, sales tax permit, food service license, liquor license (if applicable), health department food establishment permit, food handler/manager certification (ServSafe or state equivalent) |
| **County/City** | Business license, health department permit with inspection, fire inspection, building permit (if renovating), grease trap permit, sign permit, outdoor seating permit, dumpster enclosure approval, music/entertainment permit |
| **Insurance** | General liability, workers comp, liquor liability (if serving alcohol), property insurance, food contamination/spoilage coverage |
| **Timeline** | 60-120 days from lease signing to opening. Health department inspections are the bottleneck. |
| **Typical Costs** | $2,000-$15,000 in permits and licenses (excluding build-out). Liquor license adds $500-$75,000 depending on state. |
| **Common Mistakes** | Starting build-out before health department plan review. Not budgeting for grease trap installation. Missing the fire suppression inspection. Forgetting the music license (ASCAP/BMI/SESAC). Operating under cottage food rules when the operation has outgrown them. |

### 3B: Home-Based Services (Cleaning, Tutoring, Consulting)

| Category | Typical Requirements |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Federal** | EIN (optional for sole prop with no employees, but recommended), OSHA if employees |
| **State** | Business registration (LLC/corp), state tax ID, professional license (if applicable — tutoring generally none, consulting generally none, cleaning generally none) |
| **County/City** | Business license, home occupation permit, possible HOA approval |
| **Insurance** | General liability (especially cleaning — you're in people's homes), bonding (cleaning services), commercial auto (if using vehicle for business), professional liability (consulting) |
| **Timeline** | 7-21 days. Home-based businesses are the fastest to get compliant. |
| **Typical Costs** | $50-$500 in permits and licenses. Insurance $500-$2,000/year. |
| **Common Mistakes** | Ignoring the home occupation permit. Violating HOA restrictions on business activity. Not getting bonded (cleaning services). Using personal auto insurance for business driving. Misclassifying helpers as contractors when they're really employees. |

### 3C: Retail / E-Commerce

| Category | Typical Requirements |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Federal** | EIN, FTC compliance (truth in advertising, product labeling), CPSC compliance (if selling consumer products), FDA (if selling food, supplements, cosmetics) |
| **State** | Business registration, sales tax permit (most states), resale certificate (to buy wholesale tax-free), professional licenses (if selling regulated goods) |
| **County/City** | Business license, zoning compliance (retail zoning), sign permit, certificate of occupancy, fire inspection |
| **Insurance** | General liability, product liability (critical for physical goods), property insurance, cyber liability (e-commerce), shipping insurance |
| **Timeline** | 14-45 days for brick-and-mortar. E-commerce-only can be 7-14 days. |
| **Typical Costs** | $200-$2,000 for permits. Sales tax compliance software $20-$50/month for multi-state sellers. |
| **Common Mistakes** | Not collecting sales tax in states where they have economic nexus. Ignoring product labeling requirements. Not registering for sales tax before first sale. Selling regulated products (supplements, cosmetics) without understanding FDA requirements. Using marketplace facilitator exemptions incorrectly. |

### 3D: Construction / Trades

| Category | Typical Requirements |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Federal** | EIN, OSHA compliance (mandatory — construction is heavily regulated), EPA lead paint certification (RRP rule for pre-1978 buildings), DOT if operating commercial vehicles over 10,001 lbs |
| **State** | Contractor's license (most states require exam + experience + bond), business registration, workers comp (most states require for all construction employees — no exemptions), state tax ID |
| **County/City** | Business license, individual project building permits (client typically pulls, but contractor must be licensed), trade-specific permits (electrical, plumbing, HVAC require separate licenses in most jurisdictions) |
| **Insurance** | General liability ($1M minimum, many clients require $2M), workers comp (mandatory in nearly all states), commercial auto, tools/equipment coverage, surety bond (required for contractor license in most states), umbrella policy |
| **Timeline** | 30-90 days. Contractor licensing with exam can take 60+ days. |
| **Typical Costs** | $1,000-$5,000 for licensing and bonding. Workers comp premiums are high for construction — $5-$15 per $100 of payroll depending on trade. |
| **Common Mistakes** | Working without a contractor's license (criminal misdemeanor in some states). Not pulling permits for projects. Workers comp lapse. Using unlicensed subcontractors (the GC is liable). Not understanding lien law requirements. Ignoring OSHA 10/30 training requirements. |

### 3E: Healthcare / Wellness (Massage, Personal Training, Counseling)

| Category | Typical Requirements |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Federal** | EIN, HIPAA compliance (if handling protected health information), NPI number (if billing insurance), DEA registration (if prescribing controlled substances), Medicare/Medicaid enrollment (if accepting government insurance) |
| **State** | Professional license (massage therapist, counselor, physical therapist — all state-licensed), business registration, state tax ID, facility license (some states require for clinical settings), continuing education tracking |
| **County/City** | Business license, zoning (wellness businesses face extra scrutiny in some cities — anti-massage-parlor ordinances), health department (if offering body art, tanning), sign permit |
| **Insurance** | Professional liability / malpractice (mandatory in practice), general liability, workers comp, HIPAA cyber liability (if storing PHI electronically) |
| **Timeline** | 30-90 days. Professional licensing is the long pole — may require exam, background check, transcript verification. |
| **Typical Costs** | $500-$3,000 for professional licensing. $500-$2,000/year for professional liability insurance. HIPAA compliance setup $500-$2,000. |
| **Common Mistakes** | Practicing before license is issued (illegal in all states). Not understanding scope of practice limitations. HIPAA violations with electronic records. Not having proper informed consent forms. Massage therapists not checking city-specific regulations (some cities have their own massage establishment permits separate from state license). |

### 3F: Professional Services (Accounting, Marketing, IT)

| Category | Typical Requirements |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Federal** | EIN, IRS PTIN (if preparing tax returns), SEC/FINRA registration (if offering investment advice) |
| **State** | Business registration, state tax ID, CPA license (accountants), state bar (attorneys), professional engineer license, real estate license — depends on profession. Marketing and IT generally do not require professional licenses. |
| **County/City** | Business license, home occupation permit (if home-based), zoning (generally not an issue for office-based professional services) |
| **Insurance** | Professional liability / E&O (critical for advice-based businesses), general liability, cyber liability (IT companies), workers comp (if employees) |
| **Timeline** | 7-21 days if no professional license required. 30-180 days if professional license required (CPA exam, bar exam, etc. are separate from business compliance). |
| **Typical Costs** | $100-$500 for business permits. Professional licensing varies wildly ($200-$2,000+). E&O insurance $500-$3,000/year. |
| **Common Mistakes** | IT consultants not carrying cyber liability insurance (one breach at a client and you're exposed). Accountants practicing without CPA license when state requires it for certain services. Not having an engagement letter or service agreement. Ignoring sales tax on SaaS products (taxable in a growing number of states). |

## 4. Federal Requirements Reference

### 4A: EIN (Employer Identification Number)

- **What:** A 9-digit number from the IRS that identifies your business for tax purposes.
- **Who needs it:** Any business with employees, any LLC taxed as partnership or corp, any corporation, any nonprofit. Sole props without employees can use their SSN but should still get an EIN.
- **How to apply:** IRS.gov > Apply for an EIN online. Free. Immediate issuance during business hours (7am-10pm ET, Mon-Fri).
- **URL:** https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online
- **Cost:** $0
- **Time:** Immediate (online) or 4 weeks (mail/fax)
- **Warning:** Third-party websites charge $50-$300 for EIN applications. It's free directly from the IRS. Never pay for this.

### 4B: Federal Employer Thresholds

| Employee Count | Federal Obligation |
|----------------|-------------------|
| 1+ | OSHA general duty clause applies, must display OSHA poster, I-9 verification for all employees, must withhold federal income tax + FICA, must pay FUTA, must file W-2s |
| 1+ | New hire reporting to state (federal requirement, state-administered) |
| 11+ | OSHA 300 log recordkeeping (injury/illness tracking) — with industry exemptions |
| 15+ | Title VII (EEO — no discrimination by race, sex, religion, national origin), ADA Title I (disability accommodation), GINA (genetic info) |
| 20+ | ADEA (age discrimination 40+), COBRA (health insurance continuation) |
| 50+ | FMLA (12 weeks unpaid leave), ACA employer mandate (must offer health insurance or pay penalty) |
| 100+ | EEO-1 reporting, WARN Act (60-day notice for mass layoffs) |

### 4C: Industry-Specific Federal Licenses

| Agency | Regulates | Examples |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **FDA** | Food, drugs, cosmetics, medical devices, dietary supplements | Food facility registration, medical device listing, cosmetic establishment registration |
| **TTB** (Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau) | Alcohol production and wholesale | Federal basic permit for brewery, winery, distillery |
| **ATF** (Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives) | Firearms sales and manufacturing | Federal Firearms License (FFL) |
| **FCC** | Radio, TV, telecommunications | Broadcasting licenses, equipment authorization |
| **DOT / FMCSA** | Commercial motor vehicles, transportation | USDOT number (vehicles > 10,001 lbs or hauling hazmat), MC number (for-hire carriers crossing state lines), CDL requirements |
| **EPA** | Environmental compliance | Lead paint renovation certification (RRP), hazardous waste generator ID, NPDES permits for water discharge |
| **USDA** | Agriculture, meat, poultry, organic | USDA inspection for meat/poultry processing, organic certification |
| **FAA** | Aviation, commercial drones | Part 107 remote pilot certificate for commercial drone operations |
| **SEC / FINRA** | Securities, investment advice | Broker-dealer registration, investment adviser registration |
| **SBA** | Small business lending, contracting | Not a license — but 8(a), HUBZone, WOSB certifications for government contracting |

### 4D: Federal Tax by Business Structure

| Structure | Tax Form | Self-Employment Tax | Payroll Tax | Key Notes |
|-----------|----------|---------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Sole Proprietorship | Schedule C (on 1040) | Yes (15.3% on net income up to SS cap) | If employees: 941 quarterly, 940 annual | Simplest structure. No separate entity tax return. |
| Single-Member LLC | Schedule C (default) or 1120-S (S-corp election) | Yes (unless S-corp election) | If employees or S-corp: 941/940 | LLC is disregarded for federal tax unless election made |
| Multi-Member LLC | 1065 (partnership return) + K-1s | Yes on each member's share | If employees: 941/940 | Each member pays SE tax on their distributive share |
| S-Corporation | 1120-S + K-1s | No — but owner must take "reasonable salary" | Yes: owner salary + any employees | Salary subject to FICA, distributions are not. IRS scrutinizes low salaries. |
| C-Corporation | 1120 | No | Yes: all compensation | Double taxation: corp pays tax on profit, shareholders pay tax on dividends. 21% flat corporate rate. |
| Nonprofit (501c3) | 990 (information return) | N/A | If employees: 941/940 | Must apply for tax-exempt status (Form 1023 or 1023-EZ). $275-$600 filing fee. |

## 5. State & Local Compliance Framework

### 5A: How to Research State Requirements

**Step 1: Secretary of State**
Every state has a Secretary of State (or equivalent) website where businesses register. This is where you file Articles of Organization (LLC) or Articles of Incorporation (corp).

Action: Search "[STATE] secretary of state business registration" — the .gov result is always the right one.

**Step 2: State Tax Authority**
Every state has a department of revenue, taxation, or finance. This is where you register for:
- State income tax withholding (if employees)
- Sales and use tax permit
- State unemployment insurance (SUTA)
- Franchise tax (TX, DE, CA, and others)

Action: Search "[STATE] department of revenue business registration" or "[STATE] business tax registration."

**Step 3: Professional Licensing Board**
If the business requires a professional license, each state has a licensing board or division. Often under the Department of Consumer Affairs, Professional Regulation, or similar.

Action: Search "[STATE] [PROFESSION] license requirements" — e.g., "Florida massage therapy license requirements."

**Step 4: Workers Compensation**
Most states require workers comp for any business with employees. A few exempt very small employers or certain industries. Rules vary dramatically.

Action: Search "[STATE] workers compensation requirements small business."

### 5B: Common State Requirements Checklist

- [ ] Business entity registration with Secretary of State
- [ ] State tax ID / business tax registration
- [ ] Sales tax permit (if selling taxable goods or services)
- [ ] State employer registration (withholding + unemployment)
- [ ] Workers compensation insurance
- [ ] Professional or occupational license (if applicable)
- [ ] State-specific industry permits (e.g., contractor license, food processing license)
- [ ] Annual report / biennial report filing with Secretary of State (most states require, fee $25-$400)
- [ ] Registered agent designation (required in all states for LLCs and corps)

### 5C: County & City Requirements

**County level** (often overlooked):
- County business license or business tax certificate
- County health department permit (food businesses, pools, childcare)
- County building department (occupancy, renovations)
- County assessor (business personal property tax — applies to equipment, furniture, inventory in many jurisdictions)

**City level** (the most variable and most missed):
- City business license / business tax certificate / privilege license
- Home occupation permit (for home-based businesses)
- Zoning compliance letter or certificate
- Health department permit (city health departments in larger cities)
- Fire department inspection / fire marshal permit
- Sign permit (almost every city regulates signs)
- Alarm permit (if installing burglar or fire alarm — many cities charge $25-$100/year)
- Sidewalk / outdoor display permit (retail)
- Special event permit (if hosting events)
- Short-term rental permit (if applicable)
- Noise ordinance compliance (for businesses operating early/late)

### 5D: How to Find Local Requirements

1. **City Clerk or City Hall:** Call or visit the city clerk's office and say "I want to open a [BUSINESS TYPE] at [ADDRESS]. What permits and licenses do I need from the city?" They will give you a list or direct you to the right departments. This is the single most effective research step.

2. **City website:** Most cities now have a "Starting a Business" page. Search "[CITY NAME] business license application."

3. **SBA Local Office:** https://www.sba.gov/local-assistance — Find your nearest SBA district office, Small Business Development Center (SBDC), or Women's Business Center. Free counseling.

4. **SCORE Mentors:** https://www.score.org — Free mentoring from retired business executives. Many SCORE mentors know local compliance requirements inside and out.

5. **County Clerk:** Same approach as city clerk. "I'm opening a [BUSINESS TYPE] in [UNINCORPORATED AREA / JURISDICTION]. What do I need from the county?"

6. **State One-Stop Portals:** Many states now have a single business portal. Examples: CalGold (California), START (Texas), NY Business Express (New York). These aggregate state and sometimes local requirements.

## 9. Quality Checklist

### 9A: Pre-Delivery Checklist

Before sending any compliance package to a client, verify every item:

- [ ] Client's business name, structure, and address are correct on all documents
- [ ] Every permit/license listed has been verified on the official government website (not just AI output)
- [ ] Every cost listed is current (check the fee schedule on the official site)
- [ ] Every URL listed is live and points to the correct page
- [ ] Every timeline estimate is based on current processing times (call the office if the website doesn't say)
- [ ] The correct jurisdiction is identified for every item (city vs. county vs. state — don't list county requirements for a city that handles them at city level)
- [ ] Industry-specific requirements have been checked against the actual industry classification (NAICS code if available)
- [ ] Employee count has been matched against federal thresholds (15, 20, 50)
- [ ] Sales tax nexus has been evaluated for all states where the client has presence or sales
- [ ] Zoning has been verified for the specific address (not just the general area)
- [ ] Insurance requirements match state law AND lease requirements (if applicable)
- [ ] All renewals are listed with their renewal dates and costs
- [ ] The annual compliance calendar includes all deadlines specific to this client
- [ ] The document has been proofread for typos, wrong dates, and copy-paste errors from other clients

### 9B: Verification Steps for Each Permit/License

1. **Find the official source.** Google "[PERMIT NAME] [JURISDICTION] application." Use only .gov domains or verified official sites.
2. **Confirm it applies.** Read the eligibility section. Does the client's business type, industry, and location match?
3. **Check the current fee.** Fee schedules change. Don't rely on last year's amount or AI-generated estimates.
4. **Note prerequisites.** Some applications require other things first (e.g., zoning approval before business license, EIN before state tax registration).
5. **Note processing time.** If the website doesn't say, call the office. "Hi, I'm helping a client apply for [PERMIT]. What's the current processing time?"
6. **Download the application.** Review it. Are there questions the client needs to prepare for? Attachments needed?
7. **Flag anything unusual.** If a requirement seems wrong, outdated, or doesn't match what you'd expect, call the office and verify. Trust the phone call over the website.

### 9C: Common Mistakes

1. **Wrong jurisdiction.** The client is in an unincorporated area (county, not city). Or the client is in a city that's in a different county than you assumed.
2. **Outdated fee information.** AI often returns fees from 2-3 years ago. Always verify.
3. **Missing industry-specific permits.** Generic compliance checklists miss things like: music licenses for restaurants (ASCAP/BMI), EPA lead paint certification for contractors, HIPAA compliance for wellness businesses that store health info.
4. **Confusing state and city health departments.** Some cities run their own health department. Others use the county health department. This affects where to apply and what the requirements are.
5. **Overlooking the home occupation permit.** Most cities require one. Clients operating from home without one are technically in violation.
6. **Ignoring the lease.** Commercial leases often have their own compliance requirements: proof of insurance with landlord as additional insured, certificate of occupancy, specific permitted use language.
7. **Forgetting business personal property tax.** Many jurisdictions tax business equipment, furniture, and inventory annually. Clients are shocked by this.
8. **Not checking for DBA requirements.** If the business operates under a name different from the legal entity name, most states require a DBA/fictitious name filing.
9. **Assuming contractors are contractors.** Worker classification errors are the #1 audit trigger for small businesses. If you see a client using "contractors" who work set hours, use company equipment, and only work for this client — flag it.
10. **Missing annual report filings.** LLCs and corps must file annual or biennial reports with the Secretary of State. Miss it and the entity gets administratively dissolved.

### 9D: When to Refer to an Attorney

You are a compliance specialist, not a lawyer. Refer to an attorney when:

- The client has an **existing compliance violation** that carries fines or legal exposure
- The client needs to **form a complex entity structure** (holding companies, multi-state entities, operating agreements with multiple owners)
- There's a **zoning dispute** with a neighbor or the city
- The client needs to **appeal a denied application** or permit revocation
- There are **employment law issues** beyond basic compliance (discrimination claims, wage disputes, wrongful termination)
- The client is in a **heavily regulated industry** (cannabis, securities, healthcare with prescribing authority) and needs regulatory counsel
- The client needs **contract review** (lease, franchise agreement, partnership agreement)
- There are **intellectual property issues** (trademarks, patents, trade secrets)
- The client has been **sued** or has been served with a government enforcement action

Say this: "This is outside my scope as a compliance specialist. I want to make sure you get the right advice. I'd recommend consulting with a business attorney who specializes in [AREA]. Would you like me to help you find one?"

### 10E: How to Read a Zoning Map

Zoning maps look intimidating but follow a simple pattern:

1. **Find the map.** Search "[CITY] zoning map" or "[CITY] GIS zoning." Most cities have an interactive online map where you can type in an address.

2. **Identify the zone code.** Every parcel is assigned a zone code like R-1, R-5, C-1, C-2, M-1, MU, etc. The code will appear when you click on the parcel or look it up.

3. **Decode the letters:**
   - **R** = Residential (R-1 is usually single-family, R-5 is high-density)
   - **C** = Commercial (C-1 is usually neighborhood commercial, C-2 is general commercial)
   - **M** or **I** = Manufacturing / Industrial
   - **MU** or **MX** = Mixed-Use (residential + commercial allowed)
   - **PUD** = Planned Unit Development (custom rules — check the specific PUD ordinance)
   - **O** or **OP** = Office / Office Professional
   - **A** or **AG** = Agricultural

4. **Check permitted uses.** The city's zoning ordinance (usually a PDF or online code) lists what's allowed in each zone. Look for:
   - **Permitted by right:** You can operate this use without special approval
   - **Conditional use / special use:** You can operate BUT need approval from the planning commission or zoning board (hearing required, conditions may be attached)
   - **Not permitted:** Can't do it in this zone. Need a variance (hard to get) or a different location.

5. **Home-based businesses.** Most residential zones allow home occupations with conditions: no signage, no employees coming to the home (or limited), no customer traffic (or limited), no exterior evidence of business, no hazardous materials, no noise beyond residential levels.

6. **When in doubt:** Call the city planning department. "I want to operate a [BUSINESS TYPE] at [ADDRESS]. The zoning shows [ZONE CODE]. Is this a permitted use?" They'll tell you in 2 minutes.

## Final Notes for the Practitioner

**Your value proposition is clarity.** Small business owners are overwhelmed by compliance because the information is scattered across dozens of government websites, written in bureaucratic language, and assumes you already know where to look. You eliminate that confusion.

**The AI does 70% of the research. You do 100% of the verification.** Never deliver AI-generated output directly to a client. Every item must be confirmed against the official source. Your credibility depends on accuracy.

**Start with your own network.** Your first 5 clients should be people you know or people one degree removed. Friends starting businesses, family members with side hustles, people in your community Facebook groups asking "do I need a business license?" Answer that question and you have a client.

**Build the retainer base.** One-time audits pay the bills. Retainers build the business. Every audit client should hear about the retainer option at delivery.

**Stay in your lane.** You find and organize compliance requirements. You prepare applications. You track deadlines. You do not provide legal advice, tax advice, or represent clients before government agencies. Know the line, respect it, and refer out when the situation calls for it.

**Keep learning.** Laws change. Fee schedules update. New requirements appear. Set a Google Alert for "[YOUR STATE] small business regulations" and check the SBA newsroom monthly. Your knowledge is your product — keep it current.
