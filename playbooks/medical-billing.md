# Medical Billing Advocacy Playbook
## ANVIL Workforce Services — Practitioner Guide v1.0

**Purpose:** This playbook enables a trained medical billing advocate to audit hospital and provider bills, identify errors and overcharges, negotiate reductions, and resolve disputes for clients who cannot afford their medical debt. AI-assisted workflows handle the analysis. You handle the client relationship and the phone calls.

**Time to first client:** 48 hours. Read the intake script today, practice the AI prompts tomorrow, and take your first case the day after.

**Average client value:** $1,200-$4,800 in bill reductions per engagement. Your cut: $200-$1,500 depending on pricing model.

## 1. Intake Script

**Setting:** Phone, video call, or in-person. Allow 30-45 minutes. Ask the client to have the following ready before the call: all medical bills and statements, insurance card (if applicable), Explanation of Benefits (EOB) from insurer, any collection letters, and proof of income (pay stubs, tax return, or unemployment letter).

**Opening:**

> "Thank you for reaching out. I'm a medical billing advocate. My job is to go through your medical bills line by line, find errors and overcharges, and negotiate with providers to lower what you owe. A lot of people don't realize that medical bills contain mistakes roughly 80% of the time, and most bills are negotiable. Everything you share with me stays confidential. Let's go through your situation so I know exactly what we're working with."

### Section A: Insurance Status

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| A1 | Do you currently have health insurance? | Determines whether we're auditing an insurance-processed bill or a self-pay bill. Different strategies for each. |
| A2 | If yes: Who is your insurer and what is your plan type? (HMO, PPO, EPO, HDHP, Medicare, Medicaid, Tricare) | PPO out-of-network bills are the #1 source of balance billing violations. Medicare/Medicaid patients have strict rate schedules. |
| A3 | If yes: What is your annual deductible, and how much have you met so far this year? | If deductible is met, many charges should be covered at the coinsurance rate. Unmet deductible means we negotiate the allowed amount. |
| A4 | If yes: What is your out-of-pocket maximum? Have you hit it? | Once OOP max is hit, the insurer pays 100%. If the client is being billed beyond OOP max, that is an immediate error. |
| A5 | If no insurance: Did you have insurance at the time of service? | Coverage applies based on date of service, not date of bill. COBRA retroactive election is possible within 60 days of qualifying event. |
| A6 | If no insurance: Have you applied for Medicaid? | Many states have expanded Medicaid. Some states allow retroactive Medicaid coverage up to 3 months. This can wipe out the entire bill. |
| A7 | Were any of the providers out-of-network? | Out-of-network at an in-network facility triggers No Surprises Act protections for emergency and certain non-emergency services. |
| A8 | Did you receive an Advance Beneficiary Notice (ABN) or out-of-network disclosure before treatment? | If not, balance billing may be prohibited. This is a key fact. |

### Section B: Bill Details

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| B1 | How many separate bills do you have? From which providers? | Clients often have bills from the hospital, the surgeon, the anesthesiologist, the radiologist, and the lab — all separately. List every one. |
| B2 | What is the total amount you've been told you owe across all bills? | Establishes the baseline. We need this number to calculate savings later. |
| B3 | What was the date or dates of service? | Statute of limitations clock starts here. Also determines which laws apply (No Surprises Act took effect Jan 1, 2022). |
| B4 | What type of medical service was this? (ER visit, surgery, imaging, lab work, therapy, prescription, childbirth, dental, mental health) | Helps us identify which CPT codes to expect and which billing errors are most common for that service type. |
| B5 | Was this an emergency or a planned/scheduled procedure? | Emergency services have stronger No Surprises Act protections. Emergencies also qualify for charity care more readily. |
| B6 | Have you received an itemized bill (not just a summary statement)? | If they only have a summary, step one is requesting the itemized bill. You cannot audit what you cannot see. |
| B7 | Have you received an Explanation of Benefits (EOB) from your insurer? | The EOB shows what the insurer was billed, what they paid, what they allowed, and what you owe. This is the Rosetta Stone. |
| B8 | Do any charges look unfamiliar or unexplained to you? | Clients often notice the obvious problems. "I was only there for 2 hours but they charged me for a full day" is a real finding. |

### Section C: Collection & Payment Status

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| C1 | Have any of these bills been sent to a collection agency? | Changes the strategy. We shift from provider negotiation to debt validation and FDCPA-based tactics. |
| C2 | If in collections: When did you first receive the collection notice? | 30-day window to dispute and request debt validation. Even if past 30 days, you can still dispute — they just don't have to stop collection activity during review. |
| C3 | If in collections: What is the name of the collection agency? | Some agencies are known to violate FDCPA. Also determines whether this is in-house collections or third-party. |
| C4 | Has any of this debt appeared on your credit report? | Medical debt under $500 cannot appear on credit reports (as of 2023). Medical debt paid by insurance must be removed. All medical collections must wait 365 days before reporting. |
| C5 | Have you made any payments on these bills? | Partial payment can reset the statute of limitations in some states. Need to know before advising further. |
| C6 | Have you set up a payment plan? If so, what are the terms? | We may be able to get better terms, or the underlying amount may be wrong. Don't stop paying an existing plan until we have a new agreement. |
| C7 | Has the provider or collector filed a lawsuit or obtained a judgment? | If there's a judgment, options narrow significantly. Need to act fast on this. |
| C8 | Have you signed any financial agreements with the provider? | Some providers slip in arbitration clauses or waivers. Need to review any paperwork signed at intake. |

### Section D: Financial Situation

| # | Question | Why It Matters |
|---|----------|----------------|
| D1 | What is your current gross monthly household income? | Determines charity care eligibility. Most nonprofit hospitals must offer free care below 200% FPL and discounted care below 400% FPL. |
| D2 | How many people are in your household? | Combined with income, this gives us the Federal Poverty Level percentage. |
| D3 | Are you currently employed? If not, when did employment end? | Recent job loss strengthens hardship arguments and charity care applications. |
| D4 | What are your major monthly expenses? (Rent, car, childcare, other medical) | Used for hardship letters and payment plan negotiations. |
| D5 | Do you have any other significant debts? (Student loans, credit cards, other medical) | Total debt picture helps build the hardship case. |
| D6 | Have you filed for bankruptcy or are you considering it? | If bankruptcy is on the table, medical debt is dischargeable. This changes our approach. |

**Closing:**

> "Here's what happens next. I'm going to request your itemized bills if we don't have them, run a full audit on every line item, compare your charges against fair market rates, and check for any legal protections that apply to your situation. I'll come back to you within [3-5 business days] with a written report showing what I found and a specific plan for getting your bills reduced. Any questions before we wrap up?"

## 2. AI Analysis Prompts

These are copy-paste prompts. Replace the bracketed items with the client's actual data. Use ChatGPT-4, Claude, or any capable LLM.

### Prompt 1: Bill Error Analysis

```
I need you to audit the following medical bill for common billing errors. Act as an experienced medical billing auditor.

PATIENT INFORMATION:
- Service type: [e.g., Emergency room visit, knee replacement surgery, MRI]
- Date of service: [date]
- Facility type: [hospital, outpatient clinic, ambulatory surgery center]
- Insurance status: [insured/uninsured, plan type if applicable]

ITEMIZED CHARGES (copy each line item):
[Paste all line items with CPT/HCPCS codes, descriptions, and amounts. If codes are not listed, paste the descriptions and amounts.]

For each line item, check for:
1. UPCODING: Is the CPT code level appropriate for the described service? (Example: ER visit coded as 99285 (highest severity) for a simple laceration should be 99282 or 99283)
2. DUPLICATE CHARGES: Are any services listed more than once for the same date?
3. UNBUNDLING: Are separate charges listed for procedures that should be billed under a single bundled CPT code? (Example: Charging separately for surgical prep, draping, and basic wound closure when they're included in the surgical CPT code)
4. INCORRECT UNITS: Do the quantities make sense? (Example: 10 units of a medication when only 2 were administered)
5. CHARGES FOR SERVICES NOT RENDERED: Based on the service type, are there charges that seem implausible? (Example: OR charges on a bill for an ER visit that didn't involve surgery)
6. OPERATING ROOM TIME: If applicable, is the OR time reasonable for the procedure?
7. SUPPLY CHARGES: Are there line items for supplies that are normally included in the procedure or room charge? (Example: Separate charge for surgical gloves, gowns, or basic IV tubing)
8. ROOM CHARGES: Is the room type and duration consistent with the service? (Example: ICU room charge when patient was in a general ward)

For each potential error found, explain:
- What the error is
- Why it appears to be incorrect
- The estimated overcharge amount (if determinable)
- What the correct charge or code should be

If you don't find errors, say so clearly. Don't manufacture findings.
```

### Prompt 2: Fair Market Rate Comparison

```
Compare the following medical charges against fair market rates. I need to know if these charges are above the typical range for the geographic area and service type.

LOCATION: [City, State, ZIP code]
FACILITY TYPE: [Hospital (nonprofit/for-profit), outpatient clinic, ASC]
INSURANCE STATUS: [Insured (plan type) / Uninsured]

CHARGES TO COMPARE:
[List each charge with CPT code if available, description, and billed amount]

For each charge, provide:
1. The Medicare reimbursement rate for this CPT code in this geographic area (or your best estimate based on national average if exact data isn't available)
2. The typical commercial insurance allowed amount (usually 150-300% of Medicare)
3. The typical cash-pay / self-pay rate for this service
4. The FAIR Health benchmark if you have data (50th and 80th percentile for the region)
5. A markup ratio: billed amount divided by Medicare rate

Flag any charge where:
- The billed amount exceeds 300% of Medicare rates
- The billed amount exceeds the 80th percentile for the region
- The billed amount is significantly above the typical cash-pay rate

For flagged charges, state the estimated fair price range and the estimated overcharge amount.

Note: I understand you may not have exact current rate data. Provide your best estimates based on available knowledge and clearly state your confidence level.
```

### Prompt 3: Legal Protections Analysis

```
Analyze the following medical billing situation for applicable consumer protection laws and patient rights.

SITUATION:
- Patient state of residence: [state]
- State where services were received: [state, if different]
- Date of service: [date]
- Service type: [emergency / non-emergency / scheduled]
- Facility type: [in-network hospital / out-of-network hospital / freestanding ER / etc.]
- Provider network status: [in-network / out-of-network / mix]
- Did patient receive advance notice of out-of-network status? [yes/no/unknown]
- Is the facility a nonprofit 501(c)(3) hospital? [yes/no/unknown]
- Current bill status: [with provider / in collections / judgment]
- Patient income relative to Federal Poverty Level: [___% FPL]
- Insurance type: [commercial / Medicare / Medicaid / uninsured / employer self-funded ERISA plan]

Analyze for:
1. NO SURPRISES ACT (federal): Does this situation qualify for NSA protections? What specific provisions apply? What is the dispute process?
2. STATE BALANCE BILLING LAWS: Does the patient's state have additional protections beyond the NSA? What are the specific provisions?
3. CHARITY CARE / FINANCIAL ASSISTANCE: If this is a nonprofit hospital, what are their obligations under IRS 501(r)? Based on the patient's income, would they likely qualify for free or reduced-cost care?
4. FAIR DEBT COLLECTION PRACTICES ACT: If the bill is in collections, what are the patient's rights? What violations should we watch for?
5. CREDIT REPORTING PROTECTIONS: What are the current rules on medical debt credit reporting? Does anything in this situation trigger protections?
6. STATE-SPECIFIC PROTECTIONS: Any additional state laws on medical debt, hospital pricing transparency, or patient billing rights?
7. STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS: What is the applicable statute of limitations for medical debt in this state? How much time remains?

For each applicable protection, provide:
- The specific law or regulation
- How it applies to this situation
- The exact steps to invoke the protection
- The complaint filing process if the provider is non-compliant
```

### Prompt 4: Dispute Letter and Negotiation Strategy

```
Draft a dispute letter and negotiation strategy for the following medical billing situation.

SITUATION SUMMARY:
- Patient name: [name]
- Provider/facility name: [name]
- Account number: [number]
- Total billed amount: $[amount]
- Errors or overcharges found: [list each with estimated overcharge]
- Fair market value estimate: $[amount]
- Legal protections that apply: [list applicable laws]
- Patient's financial situation: [brief summary — income, hardship factors]
- Goal: [reduce bill to $X / eliminate bill / set up affordable payment plan / get charity care]

Draft:
1. A formal dispute letter that:
   - References the specific account and dates of service
   - Lists each billing error found with specifics (codes, amounts, reasons)
   - Cites applicable legal protections
   - States the requested resolution (specific dollar amount or percentage reduction)
   - Sets a 30-day deadline for response
   - Is professional but firm — not aggressive, not begging

2. A phone negotiation script with:
   - Opening statement
   - Key talking points in priority order
   - Responses to common pushback ("we don't negotiate," "the charges are correct," "you signed a financial agreement")
   - Walk-away point and escalation path
   - Closing to confirm agreement

3. A recommended negotiation strategy:
   - Starting offer
   - Target settlement amount
   - Walk-away number
   - Best time to call (Tuesday-Thursday, 10am-2pm, avoid month-end)
   - Who to ask for (billing supervisor, patient financial services, not front-line reps)

Keep the tone professional and factual. No threats. No emotional appeals. Just errors, rates, and law.
```

## 3. Common Billing Errors Reference

| # | Error Type | What It Looks Like | How to Spot It | Est. Frequency | Avg. Overcharge |
|---|-----------|-------------------|----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Upcoding** | ER visit coded as Level 5 (99285) when it should be Level 3 (99283) | Compare documented complaint/treatment to the E&M level billed. Simple problems billed at highest severity. | 25-30% of ER bills | $500-$2,500 |
| 2 | **Duplicate charges** | Same lab test, medication, or supply listed twice on same date | Sort itemized bill by date and description. Look for identical lines. | 8-12% of hospital bills | $100-$1,000 |
| 3 | **Unbundling** | Components of a single procedure billed separately instead of as a bundle | Look up the primary procedure CPT code — many include prep, basic supplies, and standard follow-up. If those appear as separate charges, they're unbundled. | 15-20% of surgical bills | $500-$5,000 |
| 4 | **Balance billing (illegal)** | Out-of-network provider at in-network facility billing patient for amount above insurer's allowed rate | Compare billed amount to EOB allowed amount. If service date is after Jan 1, 2022, NSA may prohibit this. | 10-15% of bills involving OON providers | $1,000-$10,000+ |
| 5 | **Incorrect quantity/units** | 10 units of medication charged when 1 vial was administered | Cross-reference medication charges with medical records or nurse's notes if available. | 5-10% of inpatient bills | $200-$3,000 |
| 6 | **Wrong patient charges** | Charges for procedures the patient did not receive | Compare every line item against what the patient remembers happening. Ask: "Did you have an X-ray? An EKG? A CT scan?" | 3-5% of hospital bills | $300-$5,000 |
| 7 | **Inflated supply charges** | $50 for a box of tissues, $300 for a surgical gown, $25 per Tylenol tablet | Look for line items described as "supplies" or "pharmacy" with amounts that seem absurd for the item described. | 20-30% of hospital bills | $100-$2,000 |
| 8 | **Incorrect room charge** | Billed for ICU when patient was in a regular room, or billed for an extra day | Compare room charges against admission/discharge times. Patients discharged before noon should not be charged for that day in most cases. | 5-8% of inpatient stays | $1,000-$5,000 |
| 9 | **Operating room time padding** | OR billed for 4 hours when the procedure typically takes 90 minutes | Look up average procedure duration and compare to billed OR time. OR time is billed in 15-minute increments at $50-$200+ per increment. | 10-15% of surgical bills | $500-$4,000 |
| 10 | **Cancelled or not-performed services** | Pre-op tests ordered but cancelled, or procedures scheduled but not done, still appearing on bill | Ask the patient specifically: "Were any tests or procedures cancelled or not completed?" | 3-5% of bills | $200-$2,000 |
| 11 | **Incorrect modifier usage** | Missing or incorrect CPT modifier that changes reimbursement (e.g., -59 modifier for distinct procedures) | Requires CPT knowledge. Common on surgical bills with multiple procedures. | 8-12% of surgical bills | $300-$3,000 |
| 12 | **Facility fee for office-based service** | Hospital-owned outpatient clinic charges a facility fee on top of the professional fee | If the clinic is hospital-owned, they may add a facility fee that a freestanding office would not charge. Ask where the service physically occurred. | 15-20% of outpatient visits at hospital-owned clinics | $200-$1,500 |
| 13 | **Out-of-date codes** | CPT or ICD-10 codes that were retired or replaced | Codes are updated annually. Old codes on current bills may indicate sloppy billing or intentional misuse. | 2-3% of bills | $100-$500 |
| 14 | **Missing insurance adjustment** | Full billed amount shown with no contractual adjustment from insurer | Compare bill to EOB. If insurance processed the claim, the bill should show the allowed amount minus what insurance paid, not the full billed rate. | 10-15% of insured patient bills | $500-$10,000+ |
| 15 | **Surprise lab/pathology bills** | Separate bill from out-of-network lab for routine bloodwork or tissue analysis | The patient chose an in-network facility but the lab work was sent to an out-of-network reference lab without their knowledge. NSA likely applies. | 5-10% of bills involving labs | $200-$3,000 |
| 16 | **Anesthesia time billing errors** | Anesthesia billed for more time units than the procedure duration supports | Anesthesia is billed in 15-minute units. Compare total anesthesia units to documented OR start/stop times. | 8-10% of surgical bills | $500-$3,000 |
| 17 | **Admission status errors** | Patient observed for 20 hours then billed as inpatient instead of observation status, or vice versa | Observation vs. inpatient has massive cost implications, especially for Medicare patients. Check if the patient was formally admitted or kept under observation. | 5-8% of hospital stays | $1,000-$10,000 |
| 18 | **Preventive service billing errors** | Annual wellness visit or ACA-mandated preventive screening billed with a cost-sharing code instead of preventive code | ACA requires $0 cost-sharing for preventive services with in-network providers. If the patient was charged a copay or coinsurance for a routine screening, check the billing code. | 10-15% of preventive visits | $50-$500 |

## 4. Negotiation Scripts

### Script 1: Requesting an Itemized Bill

**When to use:** Always. This is your first call on every case. You cannot audit a summary statement.

> **You:** "Hi, I'm calling about an account for [patient name], account number [number]. I need to request a fully itemized bill for all services on this account."
>
> **If they say "We already sent a statement":**
> "What I received is a summary statement — it shows a total amount but not the individual line items. I need the itemized statement that shows each charge with the CPT or HCPCS code, the description of each service, the date of each service, and the amount for each line item. Patients have a right to an itemized statement under the Fair Credit Billing Act and under most state laws."
>
> **If they ask why:**
> "I'm reviewing the charges for accuracy. I need the detail to do that."
>
> **If they say it will take time:**
> "That's fine. How long? And can you email it to [email] or do I need to pick it up / receive it by mail?"
>
> **If they try to redirect you to pay first:**
> "I understand there's a balance. I'm not disputing that services were provided. I'm requesting the itemized breakdown before making any further payments. Once I have that, I'll be in touch about resolution."
>
> **Close:** "Can I get your name and a reference number for this request? Thank you."

### Script 2: Negotiating a Cash-Pay / Self-Pay Discount

**When to use:** Client is uninsured or the insurance claim is finalized and the remaining balance is high.

> **You:** "Hi, I'm calling about [patient name]'s account, number [number]. I'd like to discuss the balance. [Patient] is [uninsured / responsible for a large remaining balance] and is having difficulty with the amount. I'd like to discuss a self-pay discount or a reduced settlement."
>
> **Key talking points (use in order):**
>
> 1. "Most hospitals and practices offer a self-pay or prompt-pay discount. What discount does your facility offer for patients who are paying out of pocket?"
>
> 2. "I've compared these charges against Medicare reimbursement rates and the FAIR Health database for [ZIP code], and several of the charges are significantly above the 80th percentile for this area. I'd like to settle at a rate that's closer to the fair market rate."
>
> 3. "I'm prepared to make a lump-sum payment of $[amount — start at 30-40% of the billed total] to resolve this account today. That saves your facility the cost of continued billing, collections, and the risk of non-payment."
>
> **If they say "We don't negotiate":**
> "I understand that may be your standard process, but I'd like to speak with a supervisor in your patient financial services department. Most facilities have a process for resolving accounts at a reduced amount, especially when the patient has demonstrated financial hardship."
>
> **If they counter with a number:**
> Don't accept the first counter. "I appreciate that. That's still above the fair market rate for these services. Can we meet closer to $[your target — typically 40-50% of billed amount]?"
>
> **If you reach agreement:**
> "Thank you. Before we finalize, I need a few things:
> 1. Can you send me this agreement in writing before I make the payment?
> 2. Will this be reported as 'paid in full' or 'settled'?
> 3. Will you recall this account from collections if it's been referred out?
> 4. Can I get your name, title, and direct phone number for my records?"
>
> **Important:** Never make a payment without getting the settlement terms in writing first. Verbal agreements are unenforceable.

### Script 3: Hardship-Based Payment Plan

**When to use:** Client can't pay a lump sum but needs to resolve the debt. Works best after a reduction has been negotiated.

> **You:** "I'd like to set up a payment plan based on [patient name]'s financial situation. [Patient]'s monthly household income is $[amount] with essential expenses of $[amount]. After necessities, [patient] can afford $[amount] per month toward this balance."
>
> **If they push for a higher monthly amount:**
> "I understand your preference, but [patient] cannot commit to a payment they can't sustain. A realistic payment plan that gets completed is better for both sides than an unrealistic one that defaults. $[amount] per month is what the budget allows."
>
> **If they insist on a minimum:**
> "What is your minimum required monthly payment? And is that based on a specific policy I can review?"
>
> **Key terms to negotiate:**
> - Zero percent interest (most medical payment plans should be 0% APR)
> - No late fees for the first missed payment (ask for a grace period)
> - Agreement that the account will not be sent to collections while the plan is active and current
> - Written confirmation of the plan terms
>
> **Close:** "Can you send me the payment plan agreement in writing? I want to make sure the terms are exactly what we discussed — the monthly amount, total balance, interest rate, and the duration."

### Script 4: Disputing an Incorrect Charge

**When to use:** You've found specific billing errors in the audit.

> **You:** "I'm calling about account [number] for [patient name]. I've completed a review of the itemized bill and I've identified [number] charges that appear to be incorrect. I'd like to file a formal dispute."
>
> **Walk through each error:**
> "The first issue is on [date of service]. There's a charge for [description/CPT code] in the amount of $[amount]. This appears to be [upcoded / a duplicate / unbundled / for a service not received / etc.] because [specific reason]. The correct charge should be [amount or code] based on [reason — e.g., 'the documented severity of the visit,' 'the procedure duration,' 'Medicare billing guidelines']."
>
> **If they say "Our coding department reviewed it":**
> "I'd like to request a re-review with the specific issues I've identified. I can also send this dispute in writing with supporting documentation. What is the address or email for formal billing disputes?"
>
> **If they say "We stand by the charges":**
> "I understand your position. I'm going to submit a formal written dispute, and if we can't resolve this, I'll be filing a complaint with [state Attorney General's office / state insurance commissioner / CMS, depending on the situation]. I'd prefer to resolve this directly."
>
> **Close:** "I'll be sending a written dispute letter to confirm everything we discussed. Can you confirm the correct address for billing disputes and give me a name and reference number for this call?"

### Script 5: Requesting Charity Care / Financial Assistance

**When to use:** Client's income is below 400% FPL and the facility is a nonprofit hospital.

> **You:** "I'm calling about [patient name], account [number]. [Patient] would like to apply for your hospital's financial assistance program. Can you walk me through the application process?"
>
> **If they seem unfamiliar:**
> "Under IRS Section 501(r), all nonprofit hospitals are required to have a Financial Assistance Policy and to make applications widely available. I'd like to get a copy of your Financial Assistance Policy and an application."
>
> **Key information to get:**
> 1. "What is the income threshold for free care? For reduced-cost care?"
> 2. "What documentation do you need? (Usually: proof of income, tax return, bank statements, proof of household size)"
> 3. "What is the application deadline relative to the date of service?"
> 4. "Will you place the account on hold while the application is being processed?"
> 5. "If [patient] qualifies, will this be applied retroactively to balances already in collections?"
>
> **If they try to redirect to a payment plan instead:**
> "I appreciate that, but I'd like to pursue the financial assistance application first. If [patient] qualifies for charity care or a reduced rate, a payment plan may not be necessary. Let's start with the FA application."
>
> **Close:** "Thank you. I'll submit the completed application with all supporting documents within [timeframe]. Please confirm that the account will be on hold during the review period and give me a contact name and number for follow-up."

### Script 6: Responding to Collection Agencies

**When to use:** Client has received a collection notice or call from a third-party collection agency.

> **You:** "I'm calling on behalf of [patient name] regarding an account you've contacted [him/her] about. Before we discuss anything, I need to inform you that [patient name] is exercising [his/her] rights under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act."
>
> **Step 1 — Debt Validation:**
> "I'm requesting written validation of this debt under 15 U.S.C. Section 1692g. Specifically, I need:
> 1. The name and address of the original creditor
> 2. The amount of the debt, including an itemized breakdown of the original charges, any interest, and any fees added
> 3. A copy of the original signed agreement or financial responsibility form
> 4. Proof that your agency is licensed to collect debts in [state]
>
> Please send this validation to [address/email]. Until you provide this validation, I'm requesting that you cease all collection activity on this account."
>
> **If they push back or try to keep talking about payment:**
> "I understand you'd like to resolve this, and we may be able to work something out, but I need the debt validation first. That's my right under federal law and I'm exercising it."
>
> **If they've already reported to credit bureaus:**
> "Has this account been reported to any credit reporting agency? If so, under the current rules, medical debt under $500 cannot be reported, and all medical collections must wait at least 365 days from the first delinquency before reporting. If this was reported in violation of those rules, I'll need that corrected."
>
> **If they become aggressive or violate FDCPA:**
> Document the date, time, representative name, and exactly what was said. FDCPA violations include: calling before 8am or after 9pm, using abusive language, threatening actions they cannot legally take, discussing the debt with third parties, and continuing collection activity after a written validation request.
>
> **Close:** "I'm sending a written debt validation request today via certified mail. Please confirm the correct mailing address for your agency. And can I get your full name and agent ID number?"

## 5. Legal Framework Quick Reference

### No Surprises Act (Federal) — Effective January 1, 2022

**What it covers:**
- Emergency services at any facility (in-network or out-of-network)
- Non-emergency services by out-of-network providers at in-network facilities (unless patient gave written consent 72+ hours in advance)
- Air ambulance services from out-of-network providers

**Who it protects:**
- Patients with commercial insurance (employer-sponsored and individual market plans)
- Does NOT apply to Medicare, Medicaid, Tricare, VA, or Indian Health Service (these have their own protections)
- Does NOT apply to ground ambulance services

**Key provisions:**
- Patient pays only in-network cost-sharing amounts (copay, coinsurance, deductible) based on a "qualifying payment amount" (median in-network rate)
- Out-of-network providers cannot balance bill the patient for the difference
- Providers and insurers resolve payment disputes through an Independent Dispute Resolution (IDR) process — the patient is not involved

**How to invoke it:**
1. If a provider sends a balance bill that violates the NSA, call and cite the No Surprises Act, 42 U.S.C. Section 300gg-111
2. If the provider doesn't comply, file a complaint with CMS at 1-800-985-3059 or online at cms.gov/nosurprises
3. Complaints can also be filed with the state insurance department

### State Balance Billing Laws (Strongest Protections)

Several states had balance billing protections before the NSA, and some provide broader coverage:

| State | Key Additional Protection |
|-------|--------------------------|
| California | AB 72: Applies to HMOs and PPOs. Out-of-network providers at in-network facilities paid at 125% of Medicare or the insurer's average contracted rate, whichever is greater. |
| Colorado | Applies to ground ambulance. Patient pays in-network rate. |
| Connecticut | Applies to all surprise bills over $75. 90-day dispute window. |
| Florida | Applies to HMOs and PPOs, covers surgical first assistants. |
| Georgia | Covers ER and non-emergency at in-network hospitals. Patient pays in-network rates. |
| Illinois | Covers HMOs. Out-of-network bills at in-network facilities limited to in-network cost-sharing. |

### Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA)

**Applies to:** Third-party debt collectors (collection agencies). In some states, also applies to original creditors collecting their own debts.

**Patient rights:**
- Right to written debt validation within 5 days of first contact (or 30 days to dispute)
- Right to dispute the debt in writing — collector must cease collection until validated
- Right to request that the collector cease all communication (send "cease and desist" letter)
- Right to be free from harassment, threats, and deception
- Collector cannot contact patient at inconvenient times (before 8am or after 9pm)
- Collector cannot discuss the debt with third parties (except spouse, attorney, or co-signer)
- Collector cannot threaten to sue unless they actually intend to sue
- Collector cannot add unauthorized fees or interest

**Violations — what to do:**
- Document everything: date, time, name, what was said
- File complaint with CFPB (consumerfinance.gov/complaint)
- File complaint with state AG
- FDCPA violations entitle patient to actual damages + up to $1,000 statutory damages + attorney fees

### Charity Care / Financial Assistance — IRS Section 501(r)

**Which hospitals:** All hospitals with 501(c)(3) nonprofit tax-exempt status. This is roughly 60% of all hospitals in the U.S.

**Requirements:**
- Must establish a written Financial Assistance Policy (FAP)
- Must widely publicize the FAP (signage, website, billing statements, ER postings)
- Must provide a plain-language summary of the FAP
- Must make reasonable efforts to determine FAP eligibility before taking "extraordinary collection actions" (liens, lawsuits, credit reporting, wage garnishment)
- Must limit charges to FAP-eligible patients to no more than the "amounts generally billed" to insured patients (i.e., the negotiated rate, not the chargemaster rate)

**Typical income thresholds (vary by hospital):**
- Free care: Below 200% FPL (roughly $62,400/year for family of 4 in 2025)
- Reduced care (sliding scale): 200-400% FPL
- Some hospitals go higher (up to 500% FPL)

### Medical Debt and Credit Reporting (Current Rules)

As of 2023-2025 changes (CFPB and credit bureau voluntary actions):

- **365-day waiting period:** Medical collections cannot appear on credit reports until at least 365 days after the account was first sent to collections
- **Paid medical debt removed:** Medical debt that has been paid or settled must be removed from credit reports
- **$500 minimum:** Medical collections under $500 do not appear on credit reports (as of April 2023, Equifax/Experian/TransUnion voluntary policy)
- **Veterans:** VA medical debt is excluded from credit reports entirely
- **CFPB proposed rule (2024):** Would ban all medical debt from credit reports. Check current status — may be finalized, paused, or blocked depending on administration.

### Statute of Limitations on Medical Debt (Summary)

The statute of limitations (SOL) is the time period during which a creditor can sue to collect a debt. After the SOL expires, the debt is "time-barred" — the creditor can still ask for payment, but cannot sue.

**Important:** Making a partial payment or acknowledging the debt in writing can restart the SOL clock in many states.

| SOL (Years) | States |
|-------------|--------|
| 3 | AL, AK, DE, DC, GA, IN, KS, LA, MD, MI, MS, MT, NH, NM, NC, OK, OR, PA, SC, SD, VA |
| 4 | CA, CO, FL, HI, ID, NE, NV, NJ, NY, TX, UT, WA, WI |
| 5 | AR, AZ, CT, IA, IL, ME, MN, WV, WY |
| 6 | MA, MO, ND, OH, RI, TN, VT |
| 7-10 | KY (5-15), MO (10 written/5 oral), RI (10 written/6 oral), WV (10 written/5 oral) |

*Note: These categories are approximate. Some states distinguish between written and oral contracts, or have specific medical debt statutes. Verify the exact SOL for your client's state before advising.*

## 7. Pricing Guide

### Pricing Models

| Model | Price Range | Best For | Notes |
|-------|------------|----------|-------|
| **Percentage of savings** | 25-33% of total reduction, minimum $75-100 | Large bills ($5,000+) where significant reduction is likely | Most profitable model. Client pays nothing if you save nothing. Align incentives. |
| **Flat fee per bill audit** | $75-150 per bill | Clients who want to know what's wrong but may negotiate themselves | Lower risk for client. Good entry point. Doesn't include negotiation. |
| **Flat fee per dispute/negotiation** | $100-200 per provider | Clients who have already identified issues and just need someone to make the calls | Scope it clearly: one provider, one dispute, up to 3 calls. |
| **Full-service package** | $200-400 per case | Clients who want everything handled: audit + negotiation + follow-up | Best value proposition. Includes audit, rate comparison, dispute letters, phone negotiations, and 60-day follow-up. |
| **Hourly consulting** | $50-75/hour | Complex cases, multiple providers, legal complexity | Use sparingly. Clients dislike open-ended hourly billing. Cap hours and give estimates upfront. |

### Which Model to Use

**Use percentage of savings when:**
- The total bill is over $5,000
- You're confident you can find significant errors or negotiate a large reduction
- The client can't afford an upfront fee
- You want to demonstrate confidence in your work

**Use flat fee when:**
- The bill is under $5,000
- The client wants predictable pricing
- The case is straightforward (single provider, clear error)
- You're just getting started and building a portfolio

**Use the full-service package when:**
- The client has multiple bills or providers
- The case involves insurance disputes, charity care applications, and/or collections
- You want to maximize per-client revenue

### Sliding Scale Guidance

For clients who truly cannot afford your standard rates:

- **Household income below 200% FPL:** Reduce fee by 50% or work on a pure percentage-of-savings basis with no minimum
- **Household income 200-300% FPL:** Reduce fee by 25%
- **Household income above 300% FPL:** Standard rates apply
- **Pro bono cap:** Set a personal limit (e.g., 2 pro bono cases per month) to avoid burnout and sustain your practice

### Payment Terms

- Flat fees: 50% upfront, 50% on delivery of findings
- Percentage of savings: Collect when the client receives the adjusted bill or settlement confirmation. Get a signed fee agreement upfront.
- Payment plans: For your own fees, offer 2-3 installment payments on larger engagements
- Accepted methods: Zelle, Venmo, PayPal, check, credit card (Square or Stripe)

### Fee Agreement (Key Clauses)

Every client engagement needs a signed fee agreement covering:
1. Scope of work (which bills, which providers)
2. Fee structure and payment terms
3. How "savings" is calculated (difference between original bill and final resolved amount)
4. What happens if you can't reduce the bill (no charge on percentage model; flat fee is non-refundable but can be credited toward a future case)
5. Client's obligation to provide documents promptly and not negotiate separately while you're working the case
6. Limitation of liability (you are not an attorney; this is not legal advice)

## 8. Quality Checklist

### Pre-Delivery Checklist

Before sending findings or taking action on a client's behalf, verify every item:

| # | Check | Done? |
|---|-------|-------|
| 1 | I have the complete itemized bill (not just a summary statement) with CPT/HCPCS codes | |
| 2 | I have the EOB from the insurer (if applicable) and have compared it line-by-line against the bill | |
| 3 | I have verified the provider's network status on the date of service (not current — on the actual date) | |
| 4 | I have checked whether the facility is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit (look up on IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search) | |
| 5 | I have compared at least 5 major charges against Medicare rates and/or FAIR Health data | |
| 6 | Every billing error I've identified has a specific, defensible reason — not just "this seems high" | |
| 7 | I have checked the statute of limitations for medical debt in the client's state | |
| 8 | I have confirmed whether any bills are already in collections and noted which agency | |
| 9 | I have verified the client's income/household size and calculated their FPL percentage for charity care eligibility | |
| 10 | All dollar amounts in my report are accurate and add up correctly | |
| 11 | I have not made any legal claims or given legal advice — my findings are presented as billing analysis and consumer advocacy | |
| 12 | My dispute letter cites specific charges, codes, and amounts — not vague complaints | |
| 13 | I have a signed fee agreement on file before starting any negotiations | |
| 14 | I have documented the name, date, and content of every phone call with providers or collectors | |

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Negotiating without an itemized bill.** You cannot negotiate what you cannot see. Always get the itemized bill first, even if it takes two weeks.
2. **Telling the client to stop paying an existing payment plan.** Defaulting on an active plan can trigger collections. Keep paying until a new agreement is in place.
3. **Promising specific results.** Never say "I'll get your bill cut in half." Say "I'll review for errors and negotiate the best possible outcome."
4. **Making legal threats you can't back up.** Cite laws factually. Don't threaten to sue unless the client has retained an attorney.
5. **Accepting a verbal agreement without written confirmation.** Verbal settlements are worth nothing. Get it in writing before any payment.
6. **Missing the debt validation window.** The 30-day window to dispute with a collector starts when the client receives the first notice. Act fast.
7. **Ignoring the client's emotional state.** Medical debt is stressful. Be direct and professional, but acknowledge that this is hard. A 30-second human moment at the start of each call goes a long way.
8. **Working a case that needs a lawyer.** If there's a lawsuit, a lien on the client's property, or a bankruptcy question, refer to an attorney. You are not a lawyer.
9. **Not checking for retroactive Medicaid eligibility.** If the client was uninsured at the time of service but has since enrolled in Medicaid, some states allow retroactive coverage up to 3 months. This can eliminate the bill entirely.
10. **Forgetting to follow up.** Providers and collectors count on people giving up. Set calendar reminders for 14, 30, and 60 days after every dispute or application.

### When to Escalate

**Refer to a patient advocate organization (free) when:**
- The case is too complex for your current skill level
- The client has bills from 5+ providers and can't afford your fee
- The client needs help navigating insurance appeals (different skill set)

**Refer to a healthcare attorney when:**
- The provider has filed a lawsuit or obtained a judgment
- There's a hospital lien on the client's property or settlement
- The client believes they were harmed by the billing practices (potential FDCPA or consumer protection claims with damages)
- The amount in dispute exceeds $25,000 and the provider won't negotiate
- Bankruptcy is being considered

**Refer to the state Attorney General when:**
- The provider is a nonprofit hospital refusing to provide its Financial Assistance Policy
- The provider is engaging in a pattern of illegal balance billing
- The collection agency has committed FDCPA violations

## 9. Appendix

### A. How to Look Up Medicare Rates

Medicare rates are your baseline for "fair market value." Most commercial insurance pays 150-300% of Medicare. Hospital chargemaster rates are often 300-1,000% of Medicare.

**Steps:**
1. Go to the CMS Physician Fee Schedule Look-Up: **cms.gov/medicare/payment/physician-fee-schedule/search**
2. Select the most recent year
3. Select "Pricing Information" and check "National" or enter the MAC/Locality for the client's area
4. Enter the CPT or HCPCS code
5. Look at the "Non-Facility Price" (office/clinic) or "Facility Price" (hospital) depending on where the service was provided
6. This is the Medicare allowed amount. The provider actually receives about 80% of this from Medicare (the rest is patient responsibility or written off)

**For hospital/facility charges (DRGs):**
1. Go to: **cms.gov/medicare/payment/prospective-payment-systems**
2. Hospital inpatient services are paid by Diagnosis Related Group (DRG), not by individual CPT codes
3. Use the CMS IPPS Final Rule tables to look up the DRG base rate for the client's region
4. Alternatively, search "CMS DRG [DRG number] payment rate" for quick reference

**For quick estimates:**
- Search "Medicare reimbursement rate [CPT code] [state]" — multiple databases and tools will surface
- fairhealthconsumer.org provides free consumer-facing estimates by ZIP code (see below)

### B. FAIR Health Consumer Cost Lookup

FAIR Health maintains the largest database of private healthcare claims in the U.S. Their consumer tool provides cost estimates by ZIP code.

**Steps:**
1. Go to **fairhealthconsumer.org**
2. Select "Medical" or "Dental"
3. Enter the CPT code or search by procedure name
4. Enter the ZIP code where the service was performed
5. Select "In-Network" or "Out-of-Network"
6. The tool shows estimated costs at the 50th, 60th, 70th, and 80th percentiles

**How to use the results:**
- The **50th percentile** is the median — half of providers charge more, half charge less
- The **80th percentile** is the benchmark used by many state laws and the No Surprises Act as a "reasonable" rate
- If your client's bill is above the 80th percentile, you have strong grounds for negotiation
- Document the FAIR Health results as supporting evidence in dispute letters

### C. How to Read an EOB (Explanation of Benefits)

The EOB is not a bill. It is a statement from the insurance company showing how they processed a claim. Every insured client should receive one for every claim.

**Key fields and what they mean:**

| Field | What It Means | What to Check |
|-------|--------------|---------------|
| **Date of Service** | When the medical service occurred | Must match the provider's bill |
| **Provider Name** | Who provided the service | Verify this is the correct provider |
| **CPT/HCPCS Code** | The billing code submitted by the provider | Compare against the itemized bill — they should match |
| **Billed Amount** | What the provider charged (chargemaster rate) | This is the inflated "sticker price" — not what anyone actually pays |
| **Allowed Amount** | What the insurance company considers the maximum fair charge for this service | This is the key number. It's the negotiated rate or "reasonable and customary" amount |
| **Insurance Paid** | What the insurance company actually paid the provider | Should equal: Allowed Amount minus your copay/coinsurance/deductible |
| **Deductible Applied** | How much was applied to your annual deductible | Check that it's consistent with your deductible status |
| **Copay** | Your fixed per-visit amount | Should match your plan terms |
| **Coinsurance** | Your percentage share (e.g., 20%) | Calculated on the Allowed Amount, not the Billed Amount |
| **Patient Responsibility** | What you owe the provider | This should equal: Deductible + Copay + Coinsurance. If the provider bills more, that's a problem. |
| **Claim Status** | Paid, denied, pending, or in review | If denied, the EOB will include a reason code. This is appealable. |
| **Remark/Reason Codes** | Codes explaining adjustments or denials | Look these up at cms.gov or x12.org to understand why something was adjusted or denied |

**Red flags on an EOB:**
- Provider billing more than the "Patient Responsibility" shown on the EOB
- Services denied for "not medically necessary" that were clearly necessary
- Out-of-network adjustments on a provider the patient believed was in-network
- Deductible applied when the deductible should already be met for the year
- Patient responsibility exceeds the out-of-pocket maximum

### D. How to Read a UB-04 (Hospital Claim Form)

The UB-04 (also called CMS-1450) is the standard claim form used by hospitals and institutional providers. If you can get a copy, it contains critical information.

**Key fields:**

| Field/Box | Name | What to Check |
|-----------|------|---------------|
| Box 1 | Provider Name & Address | Verify this is the correct facility |
| Box 4 | Type of Bill | 3-digit code. First digit: type of facility (1=hospital). Second digit: classification (1=inpatient, 3=outpatient). Third digit: frequency (1=initial claim). |
| Box 6 | Statement Covers Period (From/Through) | Date range for the services — verify against what the patient experienced |
| Box 12 | Admission Date | When patient was formally admitted (affects observation vs. inpatient status) |
| Box 17 | Discharge Status | How the patient left (01=home, 02=transferred, etc.) |
| Box 42 | Revenue Codes | 4-digit codes that categorize charges (e.g., 0120=room & board, 0250=pharmacy, 0320=radiology, 0450=ER) |
| Box 43 | Description | Text description of the service |
| Box 44 | HCPCS/CPT Codes | The specific procedure codes — cross-reference with EOB |
| Box 46 | Units of Service | Number of units billed — check for inflated quantities |
| Box 47 | Total Charges | Dollar amount for each line item |
| Box 67 | Principal Diagnosis Code (ICD-10) | The primary diagnosis — affects medical necessity and DRG assignment |

### E. How to Read a CMS-1500 (Physician Claim Form)

The CMS-1500 is the standard claim form used by individual physicians, therapists, and non-institutional providers.

**Key fields:**

| Field/Box | Name | What to Check |
|-----------|------|---------------|
| Box 1 | Insurance Type | Medicare, Medicaid, CHAMPUS, Group Health Plan, FECA, Other |
| Box 21 | Diagnosis Codes (ICD-10) | Up to 12 diagnoses — these justify the services billed |
| Box 24A | Date(s) of Service | Verify against what patient experienced |
| Box 24B | Place of Service Code | 2-digit code (11=office, 21=inpatient hospital, 23=ER, 22=outpatient hospital) — affects reimbursement |
| Box 24D | CPT/HCPCS Code + Modifiers | The procedure code and any modifiers — this is what you audit |
| Box 24E | Diagnosis Pointer | Links the procedure to the diagnosis — if the link doesn't make sense, the claim may be coded incorrectly |
| Box 24F | Charges | Dollar amount for each service |
| Box 24G | Units | Number of units — check for accuracy |
| Box 28 | Total Charge | Sum of all charges — verify the math |
| Box 33 | Billing Provider NPI | National Provider Identifier — use this to look up the provider |
